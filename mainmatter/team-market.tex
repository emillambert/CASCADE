\section{Team Experience and Market Evaluation}

MASFE is a solo submission, so credibility comes from systems execution rather than headcount. Emil Wes Lambert is completing a BSc in Aerospace Engineering at TU Delft with coursework in spacecraft systems engineering, AOCS, mission analysis, and embedded flight software. \textbf{Past projects:} the TU Delft Design Synthesis Exercise required full-mission requirements tracing, subsystem trade studies, and budget closure for a Venus mission concept, while two years in AeroDelft as Partnerships and Business Manager built experience in stakeholder alignment, commercial framing, and turning technically ambitious concepts into credible development roadmaps. That combination maps directly onto MASFE's mix of SWAP realism and market positioning.

The commercial case is a focused greenfield entry rather than a vague platform claim. A reasonable market framing is a \$12B precision-agriculture TAM, a roughly \$2.5B satellite-analytics SAM, and a sub-\$50M near-term SOM for sub-day adaptive pathogen alerting. MASFE's pricing target of \$3--5/ha/year supports break-even at roughly 500,000 ha with a small hosted constellation, while the software layer remains the defensible asset because the orchestration engine can be relicensed across missions.

That generalization story matters for the judges' business-model criterion. Agriculture is the primary entry market, but the same scheduling architecture can be retuned for wildfire fuel-moisture monitoring, flood-front tracking, or urban heat-island response with new science products and the same compute-and-downlink policy core. In other words, MASFE is not only a farm-monitoring product; it is an onboard resource-allocation engine with agriculture as its strongest first proof point.
